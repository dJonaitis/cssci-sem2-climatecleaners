Land-Stored Carbon

Land-stored carbon refers to carbon dioxide absorbed and retained by terrestrial ecosystems like forests, grasslands, and wetlands. These natural reservoirs play a crucial role in mitigating climate change by capturing carbon from the atmosphere. Protecting and restoring these ecosystems is essential for maintaining carbon balance and combating global warming.

Ocean-Stored Carbon

Ocean-stored carbon refers to carbon dioxide absorbed and stored in the world's oceans. Marine organisms like phytoplankton play a vital role by absorbing CO2 during photosynthesis. Additionally, oceanic processes like carbonate chemistry and sedimentation contribute to carbon storage. These mechanisms help regulate atmospheric CO2 levels, impacting global climate dynamics.

Rock-Stored Carbon

Rock-stored carbon refers to the process where certain types of rocks, such as limestone, naturally absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere through a process called carbonation. Over time, this captured carbon becomes integrated into the rock formations, effectively sequestering it from the atmosphere and aiding in carbon cycle regulation.

Construction-Stored Carbon

Construction materials, notably concrete, can contribute to carbon storage through carbon capture and utilization (CCU). In this process, CO2 emissions from industrial sources are captured and incorporated into concrete, effectively sequestering carbon. Utilizing such materials helps mitigate climate change by reducing the carbon footprint associated with construction activities.
